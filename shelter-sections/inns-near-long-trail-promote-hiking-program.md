# General Information for GMC System Overnight Campsites:

On March 4, 1910, Taft sent a call to eighteen prominent citizens to form a "Green Mountain Club". The stated purpose was to make the Vermont mountains play a larger part in the life of the people to awaken an interest in the mountains of Vermont, to encourage mountain climbing, to make trails, build shelter, and aid in the preparation of maps and guidebooks." [Forest & Crag by Laura & Guy Waterman, 1989]

Under Buchanan, the LT Patrol not only maintained whatever trail the section volunteers couldn't handle but did most of the major construction work as well. The crowning achievement of the early years of the LT Patrol was the filling in of the chain of shelters and lodges, so that from Massachusetts to Canada, hikers were guaranteed a roof over their heads for the night at intervals of never more than 8 miles. The characteristic Buchanan camp was a small, enclosed cabin with bunks, shuttered windows, a single table with benches and a wood stove. Though the LT Patrol worked on all parts of the trail. most of its early camp construction was north of Mansfield, where the gaps were greatest when Buchanan came on the scene: French and Barrows were built in 1931, Parker in 1932, Journey's End and Ritterbush in 1933, Shooting Star in 1934, and numerous replacements and reconstruction over the subsequent years. Butler Lodge, on the southwest slope of Mansfield, was the largest of the early Buchanan works and the one of which he was most proud. When he retired in 1967, it was estimated that under his direction and personal involvement, the LT Patrol had built 37 of the camps then in use. All built to such exacting specifications as "to within a foot-and-a-half" and large enough for six hikers or six porcupines, but not six hikers and six porcupines. [Forest & Crag by Laura & Guy Waterman, 1989]

The committee on requirements for Long Trail buildings reported by W.M. Fay that the types of such buildings seem to fall into three general classifications, viz., shelters, camps and lodges which are defined as follows: A shelter is a rough, open face building of the Adirondack type, of dimensions as stated, to accommodate 6 people at most. It has no stove or other equipment but has an open fireplace in front for cooking and heat. The shelter is recommended for an overflow near much used camps and lodges, also where the terrain is unusually rough and the distance between two of the present stations seems long, and to accommodate those who want to sleep in closer contact with the out-of-doors.

A camp is larger than a shelter and more pretentious. It is of the closed-open type with windows and has wooden shutters by adjusting which it can be used either closed or open. It should have a stove, table, benches, and a wooden floor. A lodge is a larger and more luxurious building. It is most useful in those parts of the Trail that are popular and easily accessible by motor. It should accommodate at least 16 persons, and should be equipped with stove, table, benches, cooking utensils, dishes, etc., often with a fireplace, plenty of windows, and in some cases screened. It is recommended that before the Club of any section constructs either type of building, specifications thereof be first approved by the trustees Cf a committee of them. Also, that no buildings will be maintained by the Club Cf included in the guidebook unless officially accepted by the trustees. The written report included diagrams and elevations of a shelter and a camp. [LT News April 1934]

With the Trail completed, the Club continued an established program of building shelters. Prime mover in this effect was Professor Roy O. Buchanan of the University of Vermont in Burlington. In 1931 the Club's Board of Trustees authorized formation of a salaried crew to work on the Trail each summer. Buchanan was the Long Trail Patrol's first leader. Each year he assembled a group of students and worked with them on basic trail maintenance, repairs to existing shelters and construction of new ones. During the next ten years the LTP, Sections, and others built or rebuilt 29 shelters and lodges. Shelter construction and reconstruction resumed at a somewhat reduced rate through the 1950's, then accelerated in the 1960's. Stimulating this activity in part was recognition of a factor which the Club has been dealing with ever since - significant growth in the numbers of hikers using the LT system. From 1966 to 1975, responding to the impact of the increased trail and shelter use, and also correcting what probably should have been corrected before - the Club launched a variety of programs; the removal of dumps at shelters and promotion of the "carry-in, carry-out' policy; the stationing of caretakers at heavily used shelters to maintain the facilities.

[GB 21st Edition 1977]

Presently there are over seventy GMC overnight sites, spaced no more than a moderate day's hike apart, along the Long Trail System. These range from tiny, enclosed lodges to three-sided lean-tos and tent sites. All sites, although primitive, have a water source (purity and reliability cannot be guaranteed) and privy. Visitors must carry food, a backpacking stove and overnight gear.

The cabins we built had three categories. A shelter was an open front Adirondack style structure. A camp was a four-sided structure, about 12 by 14 feet with upper and lower bunks in each back corner. Each bunk could accommodate two comfortably or three very tolerant and close friends. I never heard the six hikers or six porcupines description (a story Roy Buchanan told). Each bottom bunk was extended at the foot and the free side to provided seating. The seating on the free side served as the seating for the table which was between the two sets of bunks. This bunk and table arrangement freed the front part of the cabin for general use. A stove was placed at one end of the general use area. A camp had two or three glassed windows, one in back of the stove. one at the/ end of the table and the third one, if used, at the other end of the general use area. The other camp openings were closable by hinged, solid shutters. A lodge was similar to a camp, but somewhat larger to accommodate more people. Some of the lodges in those early days had full-sized kitchen ranges brought in in pieces on the backs of some of the more dedicated hikers. [Reminiscences about Roy Buchanan and The LT By Chet Buchanan. 1998]

The overnight structures along the trail are designated as shelters. camps and lodges. Shelters usually have open fronts in the Adirondack style. Some camps are enclosed and have doors and glazed windows. The larger enclosed structures are lodges. Any exceptions to these classifications are noted. In the trail description the word "shelter" is also used in referring to trail structures in general.

These facilities are first-come, first-served. Please fit fellow hikers in. Use of any overnight facility is limited to three consecutive nights.

Groups should follow group hiking guidelines and restrictions noted in the Long Trail Guidebook. A moderate fee is charged at overnight sites with caretakers.

To improve outhouse efficiency and reduce odors, whenever possible urinate in the woods well away from water sources. As part of its waste management efforts, the GMC composts sewage at some overnight sites. Please follow any special instructions posted on the outhouse door.

To help support this important program, a modest overnight use fee is charged at caretaker sites:

$4 for non-members; GMC'ers with membership cards pay a $3 fee except at shelters on National Forest land where the discount is not allowed. (These fees were for the 1996 season and are subject to change.) This is a use fee and applies to tent as well as shelter users.

If you packed it in, pack it out! Help by picking up trash others have left behind.

Use a backpacking stove. Wood fires are strongly discouraged and are prohibited at high elevation. Fires severely impact surrounding areas by depleting vegetation, causing soil compaction and erosion. Fires should be built only in fireplaces provided. Keep fires small. Bum only dead and downed wood. Never cut live branches or trees for firewood.

Travel and camp in small groups. Four to six hikers to a party is ideal; ten is maximum for overnight and twenty-five for day use. Be considerate of other hikers and make space for them in shelters.

Camp at established overnight sites. Use the existing shelters and tenting areas whenever possible to avoid widespread impact.

Wash and rinse dishes away from open water. Use the designated wash pit or, if primitive camping, wash dishes and "self" at least two hundred feet (seventy-five paces) from any water source. Avoid soap as it is difficult to rinse and is more often the source of gastrointestinal distress than water contamination. If you use soap, use it sparingly; a few drops go a long way.

Use the privy if the site has one. Otherwise, dispose of human and pet waste in a "cat hole" between four and six inches deep and at least two hundred feet (seventy-five paces) away from water sources. Bury toilet paper. Treat your dog's waste in the same way as your own - bury it. And, above timberline, carry it out.

Carry out old tampons and sanitary napkins. Carry a personal trash bag for this purpose. Please do not dispose of these products in outhouses.

Don't carve your name or anything else into trees or shelters. Although it may be fun to leave your mark, please refrain. It alters the experience of fellow hikers and is an insult to the careful work that went into these creations. Respect other hikers and wildlife. Travel and camp quietly.

Pets are best left at home. If you bring a dog along, make sure it is well trained and keep it away from water sources. Leash it above tree line and at shelters when you cannot give your dog your full attention. Be thoughtful of shelter mates. Pets are likely to encounter their own trail problems, especially painful battles with porcupines.

Leave no trace. Leave no signs of your presence anywhere along the trail.

WATER - Although the GMC makes every effort to locate shelters and campsites near water sources, the quality and quantity of water cannot be guaranteed. Water may be polluted, and, during dry weather, water sources may fail. Areas that are particularly prone to water shortages are noted in the guidebooks. [LT GB 24 24th Edition 1996]

# Adopters

The Club's Adopt-A-Shelter Program now has basic policy guidelines for its operation but needs somebody to assume responsibility of its administration. Under the program, GMC'ers, individually, family, or small group may adopt a shelter by performing certain inspection and maintenance services on a regular basis for a single shelter or lodge.

At its meeting Jan. 22, 1977, the Club's Board of Trustees adopted the following administrative guidelines for operation of the program:

1.    Approval must be received from the Section responsible for the particular shelter before that shelter can be included in the program.

2.    Adopters will be expected to inspect the shelter on a regular basis, and promptly submit a standardized report of the inspection to the responsible Section with a copy to the Program Director.

3.    Work by adopters will be limited to that involving no expenditure of money except by prior arrangement with the responsible Section.

4.    The assignment of a shelter adopter does not supplant the Section's maintenance obligations and responsibilities for that shelter.

At that same meeting, President Ralph Bryant announced that Trustee Donald M. Wallace had resigned as director of the program. [LT News, May 1977]

# Outhouses

Outhouses are an integral part of all shelters. Unlike dumps, they are a human necessity and most unglamorous to take care for all caretakers of shelters. Much has been written about them in shelter journals and outing logs. Many scientific minds have attempted to devise more caretaker friendly outhouses; however, I think that an outhouse is an outhouse, is an outhouse. They stink, they attract critters, toilet paper gets wet, it is unsightly, they fill up, they have to be moved, the doors don't open, they make a lot of noise at night, they are a nightmare for trail & shelter workers, and they create a tremendous amount of work. SHIT HAPPENS!

To give one an idea of the importance of outhouse along the trails and at shelters here are excerpts from the Sterling Section of the GMC Outing Log 1971 to 1977: Work Trip June 12, 1976-(2) Fred Judd repaired several holes in the back wall of the Whiteface outhouse made by porcupines - all except one hole which was purposefully left. Fred's theory is that a key attraction to the porkies is (ugh!) the human dung in the outhouse pit; they thus gnaw through the wall to get into the pit. Therefore, if you leave a hole for them for that purpose, they won't gnaw elsewhere. This, Fred did with a handsomely designed, well carpentered hole at the ground line. Further inspections will be made to determine the outcome of the experiment... Work Trip July 9, 1977- eliminated was the porcupine access hole provided on a trial basis on June 12, 1976. It seems clear to Hagerman that the porkies were more interested in gnawing on the outhouse boards than the human dung and thus the access hole was helping rather than diverting the porcupines in their continuing onslaughts and should be eliminated. [Bob Hagerman 7/9/77]

# Caretaker Program

The Caretaker Program is not new. From the early days GMC caretakers were intermittently on duty charging fees and maintaining the buildings at a number of lodges on the Tran. Today, with some twenty caretakers. The program represents a coordinated effort by the GMC not only to maintain the shelters and trails, but to educate the many inexperienced hikers through informal conversation and example.

The Caretaker is responsible for the upkeep of his or her shelter. but does not provide services that are normally the hiker's responsibility.

GMC caretakers are experienced hikers. They are happy to provide backpacking suggestions and tips, as well as basic information on the Club and the Trail. They are dedicated to the premise that most hikers, when provided with the proper information, will seek to minimize their impact on the rail environment.

There will be a slight increase in caretaker salaries from $175. to $200. on average. A continuing decrease in the number of caretaker applicants seem to indicate that higher salaries are necessary to attract and retain qualified people. [LT News, May 1977]

**Green Mountain Caretaker**  
Howard Van Benthuysen  
[Ridge Lines; Vol 2; No2; Oct 1972]

During the last summer l had the express pleasure of working for Kenn Boyd and the GMC as a caretaker at Gorham Lodge on camel's Hump. I moved into my mountain retreat during the Memorial Day weekend and reluctantly departed on August 25th.

My duties usually began around 7 a.m. though sometimes I arose before 5. I kept my guests supplied with firewood, water for cooking, drinking and dishwashing and shared my two-burner Coleman stove. Interestingly enough, there were 121 portable stoves brought into Gorham. These ranged in sophistication from Sterno, through Svea 123's (the most popular but not the best), to the heavy and bulky Coleman single burner stoves and alcohol burners. Thus, the wood stove was only used five times.

This trend toward portable stove usage on the Long Trail is especially significant to the Mt. Mansfield and Camel's Hump areas. Both of these areas are State parks. But, more important within was of the tracts are small areas which have been designated as "natural areas." In each case "natural areas means those regions on the summits which are covered with rare and fragile alpine vegetation. This designation, promulgated by the Legislature in cooperation with the Department of Forests and Parks, is designed to preserve and protect the few unspoiled lands left in the State. Thus. in both of these areas, and in fact on all State land. it is unlawful to build open fires. pick or destroy plant life or camp in other than authorized sites. You are (Probably familiar with the reasons for these regulations: overuse, misuse by the proverbial "few." fire hazard and the growing desire to retain some truly natural areas.

In addition to my routine duties of lodge maintenance and clean-up, I was able to find a handful of sunny days in which to re-blaze several of the side trails in the area, do some corduroying and water barring and remove the dump from Gorham. Another of my responsibilities was the weekly inspection and repair of Wiley Lodge, a small frame lodge 4.3 miles northwest of the Hump.

Over the course of my 90-day period of caretaking, I greeted a total of 620 overnight guests. This is an increase of roughly 100 over last year's recorded use for Gorham Lodge. In addition to 620 overnight guests, I had 304 hikers pass through during July and August who did not stay overnight.  Of the 620, 44% were from Vermont and 16% from Massachusetts. Next came Connecticut, New York, New Jersey, and Canada Guests signed in from such exotic places as Hawaii, Iceland, New Zealand, and Italy. Nineteen other states were represented.

I took the job as caretaker because I felt that in this day and age, I could not afford to pass up on opportunity to live by myself in unspoiled Vermont woods. I just did not think. that I would have many more opportunities to do this type of thing. I genuinely enjoyed my summer on the Long Trail; living in the midst of such beauty, dealing with pleasant and interesting people and having countless hours for reflection and study - all combined to make my care-taking experience highly rewarding and unforgettable.

**Reflections of a GMC Caretaker**

Peg Whitson [Ridge Lines; Vol 4, No 4; March 1975]

Bolton and Me

It was gone - much too quickly. It was not a spectacular summer like rafting down the Grand Canyon. It was a summer of peace and people and sharing. A summer; of feeling no need for spending or TV or loud parties. I missed ice-cold drinks and a good reading light. Nothing more.

I met over 800 people, only 9 of whom I wouldn't welcome back. Are people different on the trail or does a different sort of person use the trail?

It was a summer of hard physical labor - coupled with complete relaxation beside a campfire or a secluded swimming hole. I have a renewed faith in the human race, especially young people. My sense of awe was unceasing at the natural wonders of a newly discovered waterfall, a golden halo over Camel's Hump, or a moss-covered rock deep in the woods.

I feel I have served my guests well. More appreciation I have never received. I've learned to be generous with what I have. I've given food, stove, candles, and time. I have received in return, more food, money, fuel, and best of all. much good will.
